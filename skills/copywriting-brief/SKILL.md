# Copywriting Brief

Use this skill when the main angle is already mostly clear and the next job is to prepare a strong writing brief for a reader-facing piece. This skill does not write the final article. It prepares a usable brief that another writing step can draft from.

**Announce at start:** "I'm using the copywriting-brief skill to prepare a writing brief."

## Use This For

- Turning a chosen recommendation angle into a writing brief
- Preparing a public-facing article brief from book material
- Organizing a post around one reader problem and one clear promise
- Translating editorial judgment into a reader-facing writing route
- Responding to requests framed as "editorial guidance", "写作路线", "文章指导", or similar

## Do Not Use This For

- Exploring angles from scratch
- Planning an internal approval report
- Writing the final article in one pass

If the angle is still unclear, use `brainstorming` first.  
If the task is an internal report or proposal plan, use `proposal-planning`.

## Core Rules

- One article, one main reader promise.
- Start from the reader problem, not from admiration for the book.
- The brief must tell the writer what to emphasize, not just what the book contains.
- Use book material as support, not as a dump of facts.
- Keep the route specific, natural, and human.
- Do not invent proof, reader response, endorsements, or scenes not grounded in the material.
- If the desired reader takeaway is still unclear, stop and return to `brainstorming` rather than faking confidence.

Read `reader-copy-style.md` before finalizing the brief.  
Use `brief-reviewer.md` as a self-review checklist.

## Process

1. **Confirm the piece**
   Identify:
   - what kind of piece this is
   - who it is for
   - what the piece should help the reader understand, question, feel, or want by the end

2. **Lock the angle**
   State the chosen main angle in one sentence.

3. **Translate angle into reader promise**
   Clarify:
   - what reader problem this piece begins from
   - what shift in understanding it offers
   - why the book is the right resolution

4. **Build the writing route**
   Prepare the route through:
   - title direction
   - opening hook
   - three middle development points
   - ending landing point

5. **Attach usable materials**
   Identify which scenes, claims, examples, details, or arguments from the book should support the route.

6. **Set guardrails**
   State what the piece should avoid in tone, structure, and claims.

7. **Hand off cleanly**
   End with a brief another writing step can use immediately. The brief should be complete enough that the next writer does not need to rediscover the angle.

## Control Flow

After producing the brief, choose the next step based on what the user needs:

- if the angle still feels shaky or overloaded → return to `brainstorming`
- if the brief should be stress-tested before drafting → use `requesting-review`
- if the brief is ready and no review is needed → stop here

## Output Format

### Target Reader
- who this piece is for

### Reader Problem
- the concrete issue, curiosity, fear, or desire the piece begins from

### Main Angle
- the chosen editorial angle in one sentence

### Title Direction
- 2-4 viable title directions

### Opening Hook
- how the piece should open attention

### Three Development Points
1. what the first part should establish
2. what the second part should deepen or complicate
3. what the third part should deliver or reframe

### Ending Landing Point
- where the conclusion should land emotionally or intellectually

### Usable Book Materials
- specific examples, scenes, arguments, details, or chapter material to draw on

### Avoid
- what this piece should not sound like or do

## Quality Bar

Good briefs:
- are specific enough that a writer can draft immediately
- make the reader problem visible
- keep one main promise instead of several competing ones
- use the book as support for the route
- make the intended reader takeaway legible through the route even when it is not named as a separate field

Bad briefs:
- just summarize the book
- produce a topic instead of a route
- sound like advertising copy
- pile up too many ideas with no narrative priority
